PMM Report

This report will give you a hopefully insightful overview of the media you've accumulated. It will help you understand the usage of your library and how the space on your hard drive is being utilized. Through a range of visualizations and data analysis, you will be able to see the relationships between different variables such as the types of media, the file sizes, and the timescale.


Library entries over time

This graph shows you the cumulative amount of movies and TV-shows you added over time. Note that this date represents the last modified time so if you updated files recently, they'll be listed as added recently.

You can clearly see how fast or slow your library increases over the years. Depending on what this plot looks like for you, this might mean different things: If the lines grow linearly, your library grows in a constant rate. So if you had 50 movies by the end of 2019, you'll have 100 movies by the end of 2020. If the lines grow exponentially, it shows that you've started out slowly but the more media you have, the more you want to add!


Distribution of episodes

Have you ever wondered how some shows just have so many more episodes than others? Now you can check for real! This plot shows the distribution of episodes for your shows. The x-axis describes the number of episodes, the y-axis the number of shows that have this many episodes.

Size comparison by filetype

Are some of your movies using certain fileformats larger than other movies? Are Matroska files (ending with mkv) larger than MPEG-4 files (ending with mp4)? They might be, because they tend to carry more data like higher quality video & audio streams or additional subtitle streams.

This boxplot will show you the interquantile range (IQR) as the blue box you see with a green line somewhere inside, which depicts the median value. You'll also be able to see outilers as black circles, if there are any.


Quality scores

The quality score is a score that tries to determine the video quality of a TV-Show based on the length of the episodes, the file sizes and the amount of episodes. It can be described as the average size of each episode divided by the average length of episodes. This is then normalised to a value between 0 and 1. Note that while in practice this works quite well, it is not a perfect metric.

In this plot you can see all the scores for your TV-Shows with the highest scores annotated. These shows have a high ratio of file size to episode length, indicating higher bitrate, resolution or similar. While these scores are in relation to the other existing scores, you might want to consider compressing these shows in order to save storage space.


Movies and library size

Last but not least, let's look at the relation of the size of your library to the amount of media you have (currently only movies). This plot will show you the estimated growth as a dotted red line, with the actual growth as the blue line. The y-axis describes the total amount of space taken up by your library and the x-axis the amount of movies you have sorted by year.

The dotted red line shows you how your library will grow expectedly. If the blue line deviates from this significantly, you can conclude that movies added at that point are different in their size. If the blue line is above the red line, your movies are bigger than expected and vice versa if the blue line is below the red line.
